    • Overview

    Longo argues that the objectivist, subjectivist, and logical accounts of probability are insufficient in solving “the problem of induction” -- how we can meaningfully predict the future based on the past -- and argues instead for an account of probability based on normative equilibrium. This view asserts that we subscribe to predictive hypotheses about the future in order to coordinate our behavior with others towards shared interests. However, Longo believes that the logic of the neoliberal market -- that incentives informational asymmetry -- prevents us from ever reaching such a normative equilibrium and instead imposes a state of permanent uncertainty as market actors constantly try to know more than their competitors. Predictive hypotheses reinforce certain behaviors that become the input for other predictions resulting in other behaviors that in turn become predictable and so on ad infinitum.
